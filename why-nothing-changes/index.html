The Enforcement Vacuum

Why Nothing Changes

Everyone hates diving. Fans hate it, pundits hate it, referees hate it, and — as Robben's confession proves — even the divers hate it. So why, decade after decade, does nothing change? Because this isn't a mystery. It's a machine. The incentives reward the flop, the referee can't see it, VAR isn't allowed to look at it, and the one body with the power to end it has used that power approximately once, in 2002, for the price of a used hatchback. Let's walk through the machine, part by part.

Quick Facts

  • Referees rewarded about a third of dives with a free kick or penalty — and punished zero of 169 (David et al., 60 matches).
  • Even elite referees only tell a foul from a dive 72% of the time at full speed. They miss roughly 28%.
  • The IFAB protocol forbids VAR from reviewing a midfield dive, a fake injury, or a second-yellow incident.
  • FIFA's own Disciplinary Code allows 2+ match bans for deceiving the referee. No known case of it ever being used for a dive. Rivaldo's 2002 fine: about £5,000.

Part One

The Incentive: Diving Pays

Start with the economics. A dive in the box can win a penalty — the single most valuable event in football — and referees are handing them out. In David et al.'s study of 60 professional matches, officials rewarded roughly 33% of dives with a free kick or penalty: about one gift per match. The punishment rate for the same 169 dives? Zero. Not low. Zero. A one-in-three chance of a reward against a zero-in-169 chance of a consequence is not a temptation — it's an instruction.

And the penalty is only half the payoff. The other half is the clock. Dönmez et al. (2022) dissected 893 injury stoppages across 266 matches in a top professional league and found the tell hiding in plain sight: teams in the lead took significantly more injury stoppages than teams that were losing. Each stoppage averaged 89 seconds of dead time — and only 17.4% of the players treated were actually hurt badly enough to leave the match. When you're winning, going down "injured" buys a minute and a half of match time for the price of a grass stain. When you're losing, your injuries mysteriously heal.

Part Two

Referees Are Structurally Blind

It would be easy to blame the officials. It would also be wrong. Consider what the job actually asks: one referee, one viewing angle, real time, full speed — judging a collision between professional athletes, at least one of whom has spent years learning precisely how to make nothing look like something. Renden et al. (2014) measured how well humans do at this. Shown ambiguous foul-or-dive situations, elite referees judged correctly 72.2% of the time — statistically no better than skilled players (72.0%), though comfortably ahead of novices (57.4%). The best-trained eyes in the sport get roughly one in four wrong. The dive is engineered to live inside that 28%.

And here's the thing: the rulebook already tells referees exactly what to do about it. IFAB Law 12 makes simulation a cautionable offence — a mandatory yellow card for unsporting behaviour when a player:

"attempts to deceive the referee, e.g. by feigning injury or pretending to have been fouled (simulation)"
— IFAB Laws of the Game, Law 12: Fouls and Misconduct

Now put the two facts side by side. The law commands a caution for every dive. The observed caution rate, across 60 professional matches and 169 dives, was zero. This isn't a gap in the rulebook; it's a gap between the rulebook and reality. A referee who can only identify a dive 72% of the time cannot hand out a card that requires certainty — because booking a genuinely fouled player is the one error worse than missing a dive. So the rational referee, unsure, does nothing. Multiply that by every ambiguous fall in every match, and "nothing" becomes the sport's official policy. The law exists; the enforcement doesn't.

Part Three

VAR's Blind Spot

Surely video review fixed this? It didn't — because VAR was deliberately built not to. The IFAB protocol restricts VAR to four categories of match-changing incident: goals, penalties, direct red cards, and mistaken identity. Everything else — which is to say, almost all diving — is explicitly outside its jurisdiction. The video official can be staring at a freeze-frame of a player collapsing untouched in midfield, and the protocol forbids intervening.

✔ What VAR May Review

  • Goal or no goal
  • Penalty or no penalty
  • Direct red card incidents
  • Mistaken identity on a card
  • A dive attached to one of those — a penalty won by simulation can be overturned and carded
  • Proof it works: Breel Embolo, sent off for simulation after VAR review at the 2026 World Cup quarterfinal

✘ What VAR May Not Touch

  • A midfield dive that wins a free kick
  • A dive that draws a yellow for an innocent opponent
  • A fake injury burning 89 seconds while your team leads
  • A dive that produces a second yellow — second yellows are not reviewable
  • Simulation anywhere that doesn't directly decide a goal, penalty, or straight red
  • In short: the overwhelming majority of flops

The Embolo case is the exception that maps the rule. VAR caught him only because his dive happened to be welded to a reviewable incident — a tackle-and-penalty situation in the box. Move the same dive forty yards upfield and the same VAR, watching the same replay, is required to do nothing. Football built a video-review system and then carefully carved diving out of its mandate. The one technology that beats the flop — slow motion — is prohibited from being aimed at it.

Part Four

FIFA's Own Rulebook Gathers Dust

If the referee can't catch it and VAR isn't allowed to, there's still a third line of defense: retrospective punishment. And here's the astonishing part — FIFA already has the power. Article 14(1)(g) of the FIFA Disciplinary Code prescribes a suspension of at least two matches for acting with obvious intent to cause a match official to make an incorrect decision. Article 56 grants retrospective reach: FIFA may sanction serious infringements that escaped the match officials' attention. A dive, caught on a dozen broadcast cameras, reviewed at leisure, punished with a ban. The mechanism exists. It has existed for years.

Number of known cases of FIFA suspending a player purely for diving at a World Cup — or anywhere: none. The one time FIFA reached for its disciplinary code over simulation was Rivaldo at the 2002 World Cup, after he collapsed clutching his face when the ball struck his thigh, getting Turkey's Hakan Ünsal sent off. The sanction: a fine of 11,500 Swiss francs — about £5,000 — for a superstar at the peak of his earnings. No suspension. He played the next match. Brazil won the tournament. And Rivaldo's own verdict on the punishment tells you everything about its deterrent value:

"I am not sorry about anything. I was both the victim and the person who got fined."
— Rivaldo, after FIFA's fine at the 2002 World Cup (Sky Sports)
Read that sanction the way a player does. The global governing body wrote a law allowing two-match bans for deception, then — presented with the most famous dive in World Cup history, on camera, at its own showcase tournament — issued a fine worth pocket change and let the offender declare himself the victim. Every professional footballer since has understood the message perfectly: the rulebook is a prop. Other sports hand out real bans for less.

Part Five

The Culture Defense: "It's Part of the Game"

Strip away the machinery and you hit the last line of resistance: the shrug. It's part of the game. It's clever play. Winning a foul is a skill. Every clause of that defense is false, and the evidence is already on this site. Clever? Rosenbaum's data shows teams that feigned injuries frequently did not win more often — no link to winning, no link to advancing from the group. Football tolerates the theatrics for a tactic that doesn't even deliver. Accepted? Then why did Arjen Robben — mid–World Cup, on national television — volunteer this:

"In the first half I took a dive, and I really shouldn't do that. That was a stupid, stupid thing to do."
— Arjen Robben, to Dutch NOS TV after Netherlands vs. Mexico, 2014 World Cup

Players don't apologize for parts of the game. Nobody has ever confessed, ashamed, to a well-timed tackle. Robben's admission is proof that inside the sport, everyone knows exactly what diving is: cheating that happens to be unpunished. And if you want to see what a genuinely intolerant culture looks like, look at rugby — a sport with more contact, more collisions, and more reasons to go down. When simulation began creeping into the 2015 Rugby World Cup, officials announced a crackdown on players diving, feigning injury, or pressuring the referee — and described the behavior itself as "the football culture" seeping in. Sit with that. In the sport next door, footballing behavior is not a tactic. It's an insult.

Part Six

And Then There's the Clock

Now rewind through every part of the machine and notice the common thread. Why is the fake injury worth 89 seconds? Because the clock keeps running while the player is down. Why do leading teams suffer all the injuries? Because time spent on the ground is time stolen from the team chasing the game. Why is a late-match flop in the corner so valuable that it's practically a set play? Because football is the only major sport where lying down converts directly into victory. The referee's blindness, VAR's handcuffs, and FIFA's shrug all make diving cheap — but it's the running clock that makes it profitable. Enforcement fixes the supply side. The deepest fix is structural: take away the demand. A stopped clock means a flop earns nothing but a delay of the player's own match — and a tactic that earns nothing dies on its own. Frequently Asked Questions

Why doesn't VAR stop diving? +

Because the IFAB protocol doesn't let it. VAR may only intervene in four situations: goal/no goal, penalty/no penalty, direct red card, and mistaken identity. A dive that wins a midfield free kick, a fake injury that burns clock, or a dive that draws a second yellow are all outside VAR's jurisdiction — the video official is not permitted to review them, however obvious the replay. VAR only catches a dive when it's attached to a reviewable incident, like a penalty decision — which is how Breel Embolo was sent off for simulation at the 2026 World Cup.

Is diving a yellow card offence? +

Yes. IFAB Law 12 lists simulation as a cautionable act of unsporting behaviour — a player who "attempts to deceive the referee, e.g. by feigning injury or pretending to have been fouled (simulation)" must be cautioned. The law isn't the problem; the enforcement is. In David et al.'s sample of 60 professional matches containing 169 identified dives, referees issued exactly zero punishments for simulation.

Why don't referees book players for simulation? +

Because at full speed they genuinely can't tell. Renden et al. (2014) found even elite referees only distinguish a foul from a dive correctly about 72% of the time — roughly one call in four is wrong. Booking a genuinely fouled player for diving is a catastrophic, career-marking error, so when the referee is unsure, the rational choice is no card at all. One official, one angle, real time, and players trained to sell contact: the structure guarantees under-enforcement — which is why the fix has to involve video review after the fact.

Can FIFA ban players for diving? +

On paper, yes. The FIFA Disciplinary Code allows a suspension of at least two matches for acting with obvious intent to cause a match official to make an incorrect decision (Art. 14(1)(g)), and Article 56 grants retrospective power over serious infringements that escaped the match officials' attention. In practice there is no known case of FIFA suspending a player purely for diving. The closest it came was Rivaldo at the 2002 World Cup: a fine of 11,500 Swiss francs — about £5,000 — and no ban.
