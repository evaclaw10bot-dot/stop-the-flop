The Receipts

Sources

Every claim on this site is backed by peer-reviewed research, official governing-body documents, league disciplinary records, or verifiable reporting. Here's the complete list.

Peer-Reviewed Research

Rosenbaum, D.A., Saunders, W., Nelson, C.M. & Rosenbaum, B.R. (2010)

"Estimation of injury simulation in international men's soccer."

International Journal of Sports Medicine, 31(10), 747–750. DOI: 10.1055/s-0030-1261944.

PubMed

The headline number. Across 89 international matches, teams averaged 5.63 apparent injuries per game — but only 0.41 met the study's definition of a definite injury. Roughly 93% of the agony didn't hold up.

Rosenbaum, D.A., Sanghani, R.R., Woolen, T. & Davis, S.W. (2011)

"Estimation of injury simulation in international women's football."

Research in Sports Medicine, 19(3), 162–169. DOI: 10.1080/15438627.2011.556523.

PubMed

The follow-up in the women's game: 5.74 apparent injuries per match (both teams combined), of which 0.78 were definite — roughly half the men's per-match rate of theatre. Same instinct, better restraint.

David, G.K., Condon, C.H., Bywater, C.L., Ortiz-Barrientos, D. & Wilson, R.S. (2011)

"Receivers Limit the Prevalence of Deception in Humans: Evidence from Diving Behaviour in Soccer Players."

PLoS ONE, 6(10), e26017. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0026017.

PLoS ONE (open access)

Coded 2,803 falls across 60 top-flight matches and found 169 dives. Referees rewarded roughly a third of them — and punished zero. Not few. Zero.

Renden, P.G., Kerstens, S., Oudejans, R.R.D. & Cañal-Bruland, R. (2014)

"Foul or dive? Motor contributions to judging ambiguous foul situations in football."

European Journal of Sport Science, 14(S1), S221–S227. DOI: 10.1080/17461391.2012.683813.

PubMed

Referees judging ambiguous foul-or-dive situations were right 72.2% of the time — meaning even elite officials misread roughly one in four. At full speed, deception works.

Morris, P.H. & Lewis, D. (2010)

"Tackling Diving: The Perception of Deceptive Intentions in Association Football (Soccer)."

Journal of Nonverbal Behavior, 34(1), 1–13. DOI: 10.1007/s10919-009-0075-0.

Springer

Fake falls have observable tells. The paper catalogues the behavioural signatures that distinguish deceptive falls from genuine ones — dives are identifiable, which is exactly why video review works.

Dönmez, G., et al. (2022)

"Evaluation of stoppage time due to field injuries in professional football games: do players really need medical help so often?"

Research in Sports Medicine, 30(6), 593–602. DOI: 10.1080/15438627.2021.1917409.

PubMed

In 266 matches from a top professional league: ~3.4 injury time-outs per match, only 17.4% of treated players unable to finish the game — and leading teams suffered significantly more "injuries" than losing ones. The scoreboard is the diagnosis.

Governing-Body Documents

IFAB — Laws of the Game, Law 12

Fouls and Misconduct: simulation as unsporting behaviour.

theifab.com

The law on the books: a caution for any player who "attempts to deceive the referee, e.g. by feigning injury or pretending to have been fouled (simulation)". The rule exists; the enforcement is the problem.

IFAB — Video Assistant Referee (VAR) Protocol

The four categories VAR is allowed to review.

theifab.com

VAR can only intervene on goals, penalty decisions, direct red cards, and mistaken identity. A dive that wins a midfield free kick or a yellow card is officially invisible to the video booth.

FIFA — Disciplinary Code (2025 edition)

Deceiving the match official; retrospective sanctioning powers.

FIFA Digital Hub (PDF)

FIFA's own code allows a suspension of at least two matches for acting with obvious intent to make a match official reach an incorrect decision, plus retrospective powers for infringements the officials missed. No known case of FIFA suspending a player purely for diving.

The Football Association (2017)

"FA to punish simulation retrospectively" — the 'Successful Deception of a Match Official' charge.

thefa.com

England's answer: a three-person panel reviews footage, and a dive that wins a penalty or gets an opponent sent off draws a two-match ban — if the panel is unanimous. Proof the mechanism can exist inside football.

National Hockey League — Official Rules 2025-26, Rule 64

Diving / Embellishment.

NHL Official Rules (PDF, Rule 64)

A minor penalty for any player who "blatantly dives, embellishes a fall or a reaction, or who feigns an injury" — backed by a league office that reviews all games and fines even dives the on-ice officials missed.

National Basketball Association

Anti-flopping rule (2012) and the in-game flopping penalty, made permanent in 2024.

Anti-flopping rule (official.nba.com) · In-game penalty approved · Made permanent (nba.com)

The 2012 fine ladder ran from a warning to $30,000. Since 2023-24, a flop is also a live technical foul — the opponent shoots a free throw — with escalating post-game fines. The Board of Governors made it permanent in 2024.

Australian Professional Leagues

A-Leagues Disciplinary Regulations, Season 2025-26.

Football Australia (PDF)

Australia's top flight retains a retrospective "Obvious Error (Yellow Card Simulation)" mechanism — video review of simulation has been part of its disciplinary machinery since bans issued in 2010.

World Rugby

Medical regulations for match-day staff; officials' stance on simulation.

World Rugby Passport (medical / Law 3) · RWC 2015 crackdown (Rugby World)

In rugby, physios treat players while play continues, the referee stops only for serious injury, and citing commissioners review matches retrospectively. At the 2015 World Cup, officials announced divers would be cautioned — calling simulation "the football culture" creeping in.

NCAA (2025)

College football's fake-injury rule, effective from the 2025 season.

ESPN report

If a player presents as injured after the ball is spotted, his team is charged a timeout — or takes a 5-yard penalty if it has none — and the player sits at least one down. Treatment costs something. Sound familiar?

League Discipline Announcements

The Football Association (2017)

Manuel Lanzini charged with 'Successful Deception of a Match Official'.

thefa.com

A live example of the FA's retrospective diving charge being used against a Premier League player — the machinery in action, rare as sightings are.

Major League Soccer — Disciplinary Committee

Simulation/embellishment fines, 2011 to present.

Charlie Davies fine, 2011 (SBI) · Luis Suárez fine, April 2026 (mlssoccer.com)

MLS has fined simulation since 2011 — from Charlie Davies' $1,000 for a dive that won a late penalty to Luis Suárez in 2026, with a steady stream of cases in between (Pereira and others in 2024-25, amounts undisclosed). Retroactive review of diving, running for over a decade, in football.

National Hockey League (Department of Player Safety)

Embellishment fines under Rule 64's citation system.

Sundqvist fined $2,000 (nhl.com)

The NHL publicly names and fines divers — Oskar Sundqvist, Matvei Michkov, and Mikko Rantanen have each been fined $2,000 for embellishment, including for dives the on-ice officials never called.

National Football League (2022)

Saints organization, head coach, and player fined for faking injuries.

nfl.com

The NFL's fake-injury fine schedule starts at $350,000 for the club, $100,000 for the head coach, and $50,000 for players — with possible suspensions and loss of draft picks. New Orleans found out the hard way.

Journalism & Incident Reporting

Roberto Rojas — "El Maracanazo" (1989)

The goalkeeper who cut himself with a razor blade to fake a flare injury, and the lifetime ban that followed.

Wikipedia · Goal.com · CBC (ban lifted, 2001)

The one time FIFA banned a player for life for faking an injury. He never did it again — the site's exhibit A that punishment works.

Rivaldo — Brazil vs Turkey, World Cup 2002

Ball to the thigh, hands to the face; the first FIFA fine for simulation.

Sky Sports · CNN

Fined 11,500 Swiss francs (about £5,000), no suspension. Rivaldo: "I am not sorry about anything." The fine-only precedent that taught a generation the price of theatre — pocket change.

Sergio Busquets — Barcelona vs Inter, Champions League semi-final 2010

Down holding his face, peeking through his fingers as the red card came out.

CBS Sports

Thiago Motta sent off; no UEFA action against Busquets. The peek became the enduring image of consequence-free simulation.

Fred — Brazil vs Croatia, World Cup 2014 opener

The tumble that won a penalty and turned the tournament's first match.

Sky Sports

Light hands on the shoulders, a fall in the box, a converted penalty at 1-1. Widely condemned; never punished.

Neymar — World Cup 2018 (RTS timing study)

Roughly 14 minutes on the ground across Brazil's first four matches.

CBS Sports · Euronews

Swiss broadcaster RTS timed it: nearly 14 minutes of rolling in four games. The clip became a global meme; the disciplinary record stayed empty.

Arjen Robben — Netherlands vs Mexico, World Cup 2014

The rare on-record admission: "In the first half I took a dive."

ESPN · VOA

A player confessed to diving at a World Cup, on television, mid-tournament. FIFA's response: nothing.

Jürgen Klinsmann — World Cup final 1990

The airborne theatrics that got the first red card in a World Cup final.

These Football Times

Monzón's lunge was genuinely reckless — the contact was real. Klinsmann's contribution was the embellishment, a reminder that theatre and legitimate fouls aren't mutually exclusive.

Breel Embolo — World Cup 2026 quarter-final

Sent off for simulation after VAR review — Argentina vs Switzerland, July 2026.

ESPN · FOX Sports

Replays showed Embolo falling before contact; the VAR review turned the opponent's yellow into Embolo's second caution for simulation. Per ESPN, only the fourth second-yellow for simulation in roughly 60 years of World Cups — proof enforcement works the moment the rules allow it.

World Cup 2026 rule changes

Treated players leave the pitch, goalkeeper time-outs banned, expanded VAR scope.

FOX 5 DC · FourFourTwo

Reporting on the 2026 tournament's anti-time-wasting package: outfield players treated on the pitch must leave it, goalkeeper "tactical injuries" are prohibited, and the expanded VAR scope is what caught Embolo.

"Bloodgate" — Harlequins, 2009

Rugby's fake-blood scandal, and the years-long bans that answered it.

Wikipedia

A joke-shop blood capsule cost the player a ban, the director of rugby three years, the physio two, and the club £260,000. That's what a sport that takes faking seriously looks like.

City A.M. (2019)

"The Premier League's diving problem: how the FA's rules have failed."

cityam.com

The audit of the FA's 2017 rule: only two Premier League bans in its first ~610 top-flight games. A good mechanism set to a threshold almost nothing clears.

Wake Forest Baptist Medical Center — press releases (2010, 2011)

Plain-language summaries of the Rosenbaum injury-simulation studies.

2010 (men's study) · 2011 (women's study)

Source of the Rosenbaum quotes used on this site, including: "there is quite a discrepancy between the agony frequently displayed by players and how rarely they are hurt badly enough to actually have to leave the game." Also the finding that teams that fake more don't win more.
