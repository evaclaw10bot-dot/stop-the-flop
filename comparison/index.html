The Comparison

Every Other Sport Solved This

Flopping is not a football-specific disease. It's a universal temptation: wherever a referee's whistle is worth something, athletes will try to sell one. Basketball players flop. Hockey players embellish. NFL linemen discover sudden cramps when the offense goes hurry-up. The temptation is identical everywhere — the same one the data shows infecting football at scale. The difference is that every other league looked at it and built an immune system: video review after the final whistle, fines that escalate, suspensions that sting. Football built a shrug.

Here's what the immune systems look like, side by side. Note who's in the last row.

Read the last row again. Seven organizations — including three run by football people, enforcing football's own laws — built working machinery to punish simulation. The one that governs the World Cup, with more money and more cameras than all of them, ticks zero boxes.

Exhibit A

The NBA: A Price List for Theatrics

In 2012, the NBA got tired of watching players hurl themselves backward off phantom contact and did something radical: it wrote down a price list. Under the 2012-13 anti-flopping rule, the league reviewed flops on video after games and walked repeat offenders up a ladder: warning → $5,000 → $10,000 → $15,000 → $30,000 — and beyond that, discretionary discipline including suspension. Flop enough times and it stopped being a habit and started being a payroll deduction.

Then the league decided post-game punishment alone was too slow, and moved enforcement onto the court. Since 2023-24 — made permanent in July 2024 — a flop costs your team immediately, during the game, and then costs you again afterward:

⚠️

Technical Foul

Referees can call a flop live, in-game — a non-unsportsmanlike technical assessed on the spot.

🎯

Free Throw

The opponent shoots one free throw. Your dive literally hands the other team a point.

Is NBA enforcement perfect? No — in-game calls have been sparing. But the architecture exists: a named offense, an in-game penalty, retroactive review, and fines that grow every time you do it again. Football has none of the four.

Exhibit B

The NHL: The League Is Always Watching

"A minor penalty shall be imposed on a player who blatantly dives, embellishes a fall or a reaction, or who feigns an injury."
— NHL Rulebook, Rule 64 (Diving / Embellishment)

Hockey names the crime right in its rulebook — diving, embellishment, feigning an injury — and punishes it live with a two-minute minor. But the genuinely humbling part for football is what happens after the game. Since 2015-16, the league has operated a citation system under which every single game is reviewed, and Hockey Operations can cite a dive even if the referees on the ice never called it. Citations walk players up an escalating fine ladder from $2,000 to $5,000 — real fines with real names attached: Oskar Sundqvist, Matvei Michkov, and Mikko Rantanen have each been fined $2,000 for embellishment. And for players who keep diving, the league's fine schedule reaches the bench too: repeat offenses can start costing the player's head coach money as well.

In the NHL, you can be fined for a dive the referees never even penalized. The league watches every game, specifically looking for the ones that fooled the officials — because a dive that works is the most important one to punish. Football's position is the exact opposite: if the referee bought it, it never happened.

Exhibit C

The NFL and NCAA: Six-Figure Consequences

American football's version of the flop is the fake injury — a defender "cramping" to stall a hurry-up offense. The NFL classifies it as an "unfair act" under its game operations policy, and its fine schedule is not a nudge; it's a wrecking ball. Under the league's December 2022 memo, a faked injury exposes the organization to minimum fines of:

0
Minimum club fine — the franchise itself pays for its player's acting
0
Minimum head coach fine
0
Minimum player fine — assistants face the same
📉

Draft Picks

Possible suspensions and loss of draft picks — even for a first offense.

This isn't theoretical. In 2022 the league fined multiple teams, and the New Orleans Saints case showed the whole ladder at work: $350,000 for the club, $100,000 for head coach Dennis Allen, $50,000 for an assistant, and $50,000 for Cam Jordan (his fine was later rescinded). One suspiciously convenient injury; half a million dollars of exposure. Compare: a dive that wins a World Cup penalty costs, historically, nothing.

College football joined in August 2025 with a rule elegant in its simplicity: if a player presents as injured after the ball has been spotted, his team is charged a timeout — and if it has none left, it takes a 5-yard delay-of-game penalty. The player sits out at least one down either way. The genius is the incentive design: the "injury" now costs exactly the resource it was faked to gain. Sound familiar? It's the same logic as making treated players leave the pitch.

Exhibit D

Rugby: Where a Faked Injury Is a Scandal, Not a Tactic

Rugby union is football's closest relative, and the cultural gap is a canyon. Start with the ordinary business of getting hurt: in rugby, play carries on while physios run onto the live pitch and treat players mid-phase. The referee stops the game only for serious injury, blood, or a head-injury assessment. Lying down does not stop the match, so lying down buys you nothing — which is a large part of why nobody bothers.

Then there's the machinery. Rugby's citing commissioners review matches after the final whistle and can cite foul play the referee missed. And when simulation started creeping into the sport, rugby named the invader out loud. Ahead of the 2015 Rugby World Cup, officials announced that "players diving, feigning injury or pressuring the referee will be cautioned" — and described the behavior they were stamping out as "the football culture" seeping in. Football's contribution to rugby is, literally, the thing rugby holds crackdowns to eradicate.

And when someone actually faked an injury? Bloodgate. In 2009, Harlequins wing Tom Williams bit a joke-shop blood capsule to fake a blood injury and engineer a substitution. Rugby's response: Williams was banned for 12 months (reduced to 4 on appeal), director of rugby Dean Richards was banned from the sport for three years, the club physio was banned for two, and Harlequins were fined £260,000. Careers ended. It is remembered, fifteen years on, as one of the darkest scandals in the sport's history. In football, a faked injury is a Tuesday.

The Kicker

Football Already Knows How. FIFA Just Won't.

Here's the part that removes FIFA's last excuse. You could argue basketball and hockey are different games, different cultures, different economics. Fine. But retrospective punishment for diving has already been tested inside football, on football pitches, under football's own laws — and it works. The failure isn't a football problem. It's a FIFA problem.

⚽ Football already knows how

  • England's FA has issued retrospective two-match bans for "Successful Deception of a Match Official" since 2017
  • Bailey Wright: first player banned under the rule (Nov 2017)
  • Oumar Niasse: first Premier League player banned (Nov 2017)
  • Manuel Lanzini (Dec 2017) and Eveliina Summanen (Feb 2023) followed
  • The Scottish FA and Australia's A-League also hand out retrospective two-match bans for simulation
  • MLS has fined simulation since 2011 — starting with Charlie Davies's $1,000 fine for a dive that won a penalty
  • Still active in 2026: MLS fined Luis Suárez for simulation in April

🌍 FIFA, meanwhile

  • Its own Disciplinary Code (Art. 14(1)(g), 2025 edition) allows a ban of at least two matches for deceiving the referee
  • Art. 56 grants retrospective power over "serious infringements which have escaped the match officials' attention"
  • Both powers sit unused against divers
  • The only World Cup punishment for simulation, ever: Rivaldo's CHF 11,500 fine in 2002 — no ban
  • No known FIFA suspension for diving. Not one. Ever.
  • VAR can't fill the gap — it may only review goals, penalties, direct reds, and mistaken identity

Sit with that for a second. FIFA wrote itself the power to ban divers for two matches or more. It wrote itself the power to punish retrospectively what the referee missed. The legislation is finished, printed, and in force. The FIFA Disciplinary Code is not missing a paragraph — it's missing a spine. Meanwhile the Championship, Scottish football, the A-League, and MLS — operating on a fraction of FIFA's budget — review the tape and hand down the bans. The hall of shame exists because the sport's governing body chooses, year after year, not to open its own rulebook.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the NBA fine players for flopping? +

Yes, since 2012-13 — the original ladder ran warning → $5,000 → $10,000 → $15,000 → $30,000, then discretionary discipline including suspension. Since 2023-24 (made permanent in July 2024), a flop also draws an in-game technical foul with one free throw for the opponent, plus post-game fines starting at $2,000 and escalating for repeat offenders. Flopping in the NBA has a published price.

Can football players be banned for diving? +

Yes — just not by FIFA. Since 2017-18, England's FA has issued retrospective two-match bans where there is clear and overwhelming evidence that a dive won a penalty or got an opponent dismissed; Bailey Wright was the first banned, Oumar Niasse the first in the Premier League, with Manuel Lanzini and Eveliina Summanen following. The Scottish FA and the A-League run similar retrospective bans. FIFA's own Disciplinary Code allows bans of at least two matches for deceiving the referee — but there is no known case of FIFA ever using it against a diver.

Which leagues punish simulation retroactively? +

Nearly all of the ones on this page. The NBA reviews and fines flops post-game. The NHL reviews every game and cites embellishment the referees missed. The NFL reviews fake-injury incidents and fines clubs, coaches, and players. Rugby's citing commissioners review matches after full time. Inside football: the English FA, Scottish FA, A-League, and MLS all act retrospectively. FIFA holds the identical power on paper — Art. 56 of its Disciplinary Code covers infringements that escaped the match officials — and is not known to have ever aimed it at a dive.

Does FIFA punish diving at World Cups? +

Effectively never. The only known World Cup punishment for simulation is Rivaldo's CHF 11,500 fine (roughly £5,000) for the 2002 face-clutch — no suspension, and he kept playing. There is no known case of FIFA suspending any player purely for diving, despite its Disciplinary Code allowing 2+ match bans for exactly that. And VAR can't compensate: it may only intervene on goals, penalty decisions, direct red cards, and mistaken identity, so a dive anywhere else on the pitch is structurally invisible to review. More on that in Why Nothing Changes.
