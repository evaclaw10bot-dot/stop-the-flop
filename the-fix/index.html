The Fix

The Fix: Make Flopping a Losing Bet

Nothing on this page is speculative. Every single piece of the fix below is already running, right now, in some professional league somewhere on Earth. Retroactive video review? England, Scotland, the US, Australia. Escalating fines? The NBA and NHL. Making treatment cost you time on the pitch? Trialed at this very World Cup. Football doesn't need to invent anything. It needs to assemble what already works.

Here's the four-part plan — plus the structural change that makes all of it stick.

🎥

1. Retroactive Review, Every Match

Copy England's FA: a panel of a former match official, a former manager, and a former player reviews footage independently, and a charge is issued only on a unanimous verdict of "clear and overwhelming evidence". Then copy the NHL: the league office reviews every game and cites dives the referees missed. Precedent: the FA since 2017, the NHL, MLS, Australia's A-League, and the Scottish FA.

📈

2. An Escalating Ladder

Copy the NBA: its original anti-flopping rule went warning → $5,000 → $10,000 → $15,000 → $30,000 → discretionary discipline including suspension. The genius is the slope. Your first flop is cheap. Your fifth is expensive. Your tenth is career-threatening. Habitual divers aren't unlucky — they're repeat offenders, and the ladder is built for exactly that.

🚑

3. Treatment Means You Leave

This World Cup already trialed it: an outfield player treated on the pitch has to leave and can only return a minute after the restart. Strengthen it to a fixed three minutes off. If the injury is real, you need the treatment anyway. If it isn't, your team plays short because of your acting. Either way, the incentive to writhe dies on the spot.

📢

4. Publish Everything

Panel verdicts. Citation counts. Per-player simulation records. The NHL announces embellishment fines publicly, by name — and nobody wants their name on that list. Shame works on footballers too: Robben confessed his 2014 dive on national television because even players know it's embarrassing. Give the embarrassment a league table.

The rules for all of this already exist at the global level. IFAB's Law 12 makes simulation a cautionable offence, and FIFA's own Disciplinary Code allows bans of at least two matches for deceiving a match official — retroactively. Nothing above requires a law change. It requires enforcement.

The Receipts

Every Piece Has Already Worked

This isn't a thought experiment. Punishment has been tried against simulation — rarely, reluctantly, in scattered places — and every time it's been tried, it has worked.

World Cup 2026 — five days ago. This tournament expanded VAR's scope so simulation could be punished on review, and in the quarterfinal Breel Embolo went down before contact, the review caught it, and his second yellow for simulation sent him off. ESPN could find only three other players in roughly 60 years of World Cups punished that way. Give the officials the tools, and the tools get used.
1989 — the one lifetime ban in history. Roberto Rojas cut himself with a hidden razor blade to fake a flare injury, and FIFA banned him for life. His faking career ended that day, permanently. It is the only time football ever brought its full punishment to bear on simulation — and it had a 100% success rate.
England, 2017 — divers named and shamed. The FA's retrospective panel banned Oumar Niasse and charged Manuel Lanzini in its very first season, each case making national news. The bans have been rare — the unanimity bar is deliberately brutal — but every diving charge is now a two-match ban and a headline with your name in it. That's a real price where there used to be none.
The NBA — flopping left the headlines. Since the 2012–13 fine ladder, and especially since the in-game flopping technical (a free throw for the opponent, made permanent in 2024), flopping has largely faded from the NBA's headlines. The league that once had a flopping discourse now has a flopping rule. Those turn out to be substitutes.

The Root Cause

The Structural Fix: Stop the Clock

Every fix above treats a symptom. Here's the disease: football's clock never stops. When a player goes down clutching his shin, the referee's watch keeps running — and the seconds he burns are only ever partially added back. That means lying on the ground is profitable even when nobody is fooled. The dive doesn't need to win a free kick. It doesn't need to fool a single official. It kills time, and killed time is a real, bankable reward for the team protecting a lead.

Now stop the clock on every dead ball, the way basketball, hockey, rugby, and FIFA's own futsal already do. The moment play stops, time stops. Writhing gains zero seconds. The single most reliable payoff of flopping — the one no referee can take away under the current laws — vanishes by design. Review catches the cheats; a stopped clock makes the cheating pointless.

The Pushback

Addressing the Objections

"Won't retroactive bans punish honest players who exaggerate contact?" +

No — the working systems are built to acquit marginal cases. England's FA only charges when a three-person panel — a former match official, a former manager, and a former player, each reviewing the footage independently — unanimously finds "clear and overwhelming evidence" of deception, and only where that deception directly won a penalty or got an opponent sent off. Anything ambiguous walks free. The bar is deliberately set so high that only the theatrical, contact-free collapses get caught.

"Isn't diving just clever gamesmanship?" +

The data says it isn't even clever. Rosenbaum's peer-reviewed study found no link between frequent faking and results: "There was no evidence that teams that did this frequently won more often." No link to winning, no link to advancing from the group. All of the shame, none of the payoff. And other sports don't admire the craft — when rugby officials cracked down on simulation before their 2015 World Cup, they described it as "the football culture" creeping into their game. That's our sport, used as an insult.

"Would players stop drawing legitimate fouls?" +

No. Drawing a real foul stays completely legal — only deception is punished. The FA's offence is literally named "successful deception of a match official," not "going down under contact." And we have a decade-plus of evidence from across the Atlantic: the NBA and NHL both punish flopping and embellishment, and both leagues are still full of drawn charges, drawn penalties, and contact plays. Punishing lying doesn't punish playing.

"Hasn't football already tried this?" +

Yes — and the trials work, which is exactly the point. The FA has issued retrospective simulation bans since 2017. The Scottish FA has had a retrospective simulation offence since 2011. MLS's Disciplinary Committee has fined simulation since 2011 — and was still doing it in April 2026. Australia's A-League handed out two-match bans after video review back in 2010. All of these systems are still running. What's missing isn't proof of concept. It's FIFA scaling any of them to the game's biggest stages.

"Why hasn't FIFA done this already?" +

Here's the maddening part: FIFA already has the law. Its own Disciplinary Code — Art. 14(1)(g) — prescribes a suspension of at least two matches for acting with obvious intent to cause a match official to make an incorrect decision, and Art. 56(2) gives FIFA retrospective power over serious infringements that escaped the officials' attention. Yet there is no known case of FIFA ever suspending a player purely for diving. The closest it came was fining Rivaldo about £5,000 in 2002 — no ban, and he won the World Cup that summer. What's missing is will, not law.

The whole fix, in one sentence: review every match, escalate the punishments, make treatment cost time on the pitch, publish the records — and stop the clock, so that even the flops nobody catches gain nothing at all.
