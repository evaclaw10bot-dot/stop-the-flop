The All-Time Greats of Fake Agony

The Hall of Shame

What follows is a chronological gallery of football's greatest performances that never touched a stage. Four decades of face-clutching, multi-roll tumbling, and career-defining agony — inflicted, in most cases, by absolutely nobody. These aren't obscure incidents. Every one of them happened on the biggest broadcasts in world sport, in front of hundreds of millions of people. The researchers who counted found 93% of football "injuries" don't hold up to scrutiny; this page is what the other 93% looks like in its Sunday best.

As you scroll, watch for the pattern. The crime is always visible — replayed, memed, laughed at on every continent within the hour. The punishment almost never comes. Out of eight entries below, exactly two ended in real consequences. One of them took a razor blade. The other took until five days ago.

1989 — Roberto Rojas cuts himself with a razor blade

The anchor exhibit, because it's the one time football actually did something. September 3, 1989, World Cup qualifier, the Maracanã: Brazil lead Chile 1-0 around the 70th minute when a flare thrown from the crowd lands about a yard from Chilean goalkeeper Roberto Rojas. Rojas collapses, writhing, blood streaming from his forehead. Chile refuse to continue and the match is abandoned — a result that could have put Brazil out of the 1990 World Cup. Then photographer Ricardo Alfieri's pictures showed the flare never touched him. Rojas confessed: he had cut his own forehead with a razor blade concealed in his glove. (Wikipedia)

"I cut myself with a razor... I cut my own dignity."
— Roberto Rojas, years later, to Goal.com

Brazil were awarded a 2-0 win. Chile were barred from qualifying for the 1994 World Cup. Rojas received a FIFA lifetime ban — lifted only in 2001, as a pardon, long after his career was over. He never played again. Remember this one. It's the control group.

Punishment: lifetime ban. The one time football went all the way — and, funny enough, the one player who never did it again.

1990 — Jürgen Klinsmann turns a real foul into an air show

July 8, 1990, World Cup final, Rome. In the 65th minute, Argentina's Pedro Monzón goes into Klinsmann studs-up — a genuinely reckless lunge, let's be fair. What follows, however, is not physics. Klinsmann goes airborne, rotating, and keeps rolling long after the ground has finished with him. Monzón becomes the first player ever sent off in a World Cup final. The tackle was real; the crime here was the embellishment — taking legitimate contact and performing it into legend. (These Football Times)

Punishment: none. The embellishment worked, the red card stood, and West Germany won the World Cup.

2002 — Rivaldo's thigh injures his face

June 3, 2002, World Cup group stage, Brazil 2-1 Turkey. In injury time, Turkey's Hakan Ünsal kicks the ball at Rivaldo, who is waiting at the corner flag. The ball hits his thigh. Rivaldo collapses clutching his face — a part of his body the ball had visibly declined to visit. Ünsal is shown a second yellow and sent off. FIFA's disciplinary committee, operating its brand-new 2002 anti-simulation crackdown, made Rivaldo the first player ever punished under it: a fine of 11,500 Swiss francs — about £5,000 — and no suspension. For a World Cup winner, roughly the price of a nice watch.

"I am not sorry about anything. I was both the victim and the person who got fined."
— Rivaldo, remorse fully installed (Sky Sports)
Punishment: a fine of about £5,000. No ban. He played Brazil's next match. Brazil won the World Cup.

2010 — Sergio Busquets peeks through his fingers

April 28, 2010, Champions League semifinal, Camp Nou. Inter's Thiago Motta raises an arm toward Sergio Busquets' face. Busquets goes down clutching his face like a man who has just taken shrapnel — and then, in the moment that made this one immortal, the cameras catch him parting his fingers to peek at referee Frank De Bleeckere, checking whether the performance was landing. It was: Motta was shown a straight red in the 28th minute. The peek was broadcast to the planet in slow motion. UEFA took no action against Busquets.

Punishment: none. Not even for the peek. Especially not for the peek.

2014 — Fred opens a World Cup with a sit-down

June 12, 2014, the opening match of Brazil's home World Cup, São Paulo. Score 1-1 against Croatia in the 71st minute: Dejan Lovren places his hands — lightly, in the manner of a man steadying a ladder — on Fred's shoulders in the box. Fred folds to the turf. Referee Yuichi Nishimura points to the spot; Neymar converts; Brazil go 2-1 up and win 3-1. The dive was condemned worldwide before full-time. Fred's defense: "It was a clear penalty." (Sky Sports) The entire tournament's opening result turned on it.

Punishment: none. The penalty stood, the win stood, and the World Cup opened on a flop.

2014 — Arjen Robben confesses on live television

June 29, 2014, round of 16, Fortaleza. The Netherlands beat Mexico 2-1 with a stoppage-time penalty won by Arjen Robben (there was contact from Rafael Márquez on that one). But afterwards, on Dutch national broadcaster NOS, Robben did something no one was prepared for. He told the truth.

"In the first half I took a dive, and I really shouldn't do that. That was a stupid, stupid thing to do."
— Arjen Robben, on NOS TV (VOA)

A signed confession, essentially, delivered to a camera at a World Cup. Mexico coach Miguel Herrera accused him of three dives and demanded a sanction. FIFA's response: nothing. No fine, no ban, no hearing. In 2017 Robben told ESPN he was "glad" he admitted it. He remains the only exhibit in this hall who turned himself in — and it changed the outcome not at all.

Punishment: none — even with a confession. Football couldn't convict a man who pleaded guilty.

2018 — Neymar spends 14 minutes of a World Cup on the ground

Not one incident — a body of work. At the 2018 World Cup, Swiss broadcaster RTS totted up the tape and found Neymar spent nearly 14 minutes on the ground across Brazil's first four matches (he was fouled 23 times, to be fair — but not for 14 minutes' worth). The ledger:

vs Switzerland — 3:40  ·  vs Costa Rica — 2:46  ·  vs Serbia — 1:56  ·  vs Mexico — 5:29

The crown jewel came in the 71st minute against Mexico, when Miguel Layún stepped on his ankle and Neymar rolled for roughly two minutes. The clip escaped football entirely: kids' teams worldwide began collapsing and rolling in unison on a whistle — the "Neymar challenge" — making him perhaps the first player whose diving became a youth training drill. FIFA's disciplinary response to the most-mocked simulation campaign in World Cup history: nothing at all.

Punishment: none. Fourteen minutes of ground time, zero minutes of suspension. The meme was the only sentence served.

2026 — Breel Embolo, sent off for diving. Live. At a World Cup.

And then, five days ago, the twist ending. July 12, 2026, World Cup quarterfinal, Kansas City. Switzerland have just equalized against Argentina at 1-1 when Breel Embolo goes down under a Leandro Paredes tackle — and the referee books Paredes. Then VAR, operating under the tournament's expanded review rules, looks again: the replays show Embolo falling before contact. The yellow to Paredes is overturned. Embolo is shown a yellow for simulation instead — his second of the match — and sent off in tears. Argentina score twice against ten men and win 3-1 after extra time. Per ESPN, he is only the fourth player in roughly 60 years of World Cups to receive a second yellow for simulation. Swiss coach Murat Yakin called the decision "absolutely incomprehensible." The tape disagreed.

Punishment: red card, in the match, the moment it happened. Thirty-seven years after Rojas, enforcement finally caught a dive live — proof that it's possible the moment the rules allow it.
Roberto Rojas is the proof that punishment works. The one player football ever punished severely for faking an injury never faked another one. Granted, that's partly because he never played again, period — but you'll notice the deterrent held for the rest of his life. Meanwhile, everyone fined pocket change or punished with nothing at all kept right on diving. It's almost as if incentives matter.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most famous dive in football history? +

Rivaldo's 2002 face-clutch is probably the most famous: the ball hit his thigh, and he collapsed holding his face, getting Turkey's Hakan Ünsal sent off. The most extreme fake injury belongs to Roberto Rojas, the Chilean goalkeeper who cut his own forehead with a razor blade hidden in his glove in 1989 to blame a flare from the crowd.

Was Rivaldo punished for his 2002 World Cup dive? +

Barely. FIFA fined him 11,500 Swiss francs — about £5,000 — with no suspension, making him the first player punished under FIFA's 2002 anti-simulation crackdown. He played Brazil's next match, declared himself "not sorry about anything," and won the World Cup.

How long did Neymar spend on the ground at the 2018 World Cup? +

Nearly 14 minutes across Brazil's first four matches, according to Swiss broadcaster RTS: 3:40 vs Switzerland, 2:46 vs Costa Rica, 1:56 vs Serbia, and 5:29 vs Mexico. He was not punished; the clips became the viral "Neymar challenge" meme instead.

Has a player ever been sent off for diving at a World Cup? +

Yes — most recently on July 12, 2026, when Switzerland's Breel Embolo was sent off in the quarterfinal against Argentina after VAR showed he fell before contact. It was his second yellow for simulation; per ESPN, he's only the fourth player in roughly 60 years of World Cups to get one. Proof that enforcement works when the rules allow it.

Has anyone ever admitted to diving? +

Arjen Robben did, on Dutch national television, after the Netherlands beat Mexico at the 2014 World Cup: "In the first half I took a dive, and I really shouldn't do that." FIFA took no action — even with a confession broadcast to the world. In 2017 he told ESPN he was glad he'd admitted it.
